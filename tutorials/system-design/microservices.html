Microservices Architecture Fundamentals

Microservices trade a single deployable's simplicity for TEAM-LEVEL independence — the ability for many teams to build, deploy, and scale their piece of the system without coordinating with everyone else. That trade only pays off past a certain organizational size; below it, the overhead dominates.

Monolith vs Microservices — the Honest Trade-off

MonolithMicroservices
DeploymentOne unit, all-or-nothingIndependent, per-service
Team scalingMany teams touching one codebase → coordination overheadTeams own services end-to-end — deploy independently
Failure isolationOne bug can crash the whole appA failing service can be isolated (with proper resilience patterns)
Operational complexityLow — one thing to run, one log streamHigh — distributed tracing, service discovery, network reliability all become YOUR problem
Data consistencyACID transactions across the whole appNo cross-service transactions — need sagas or eventual consistency
⚠️ Interview trap: "microservices are just better." They're not — they trade simplicity for independence, and that trade only makes sense once a SINGLE codebase becomes the bottleneck for MULTIPLE teams. A 5-person startup adopting microservices on day one is usually paying distributed-systems tax (network calls, partial failures, eventual consistency) for a problem it doesn't have yet. "Start with a modular monolith, split when team/scale pain is REAL" is the mature interview answer.

Service Boundaries — the Hardest Design Decision

Bad boundaries create a "distributed monolith" — services so tightly coupled they must deploy together anyway, but now pay network overhead too. The standard heuristic is Domain-Driven Design's bounded contexts: a service should own a cohesive business capability (Orders, Inventory, Payments) with its OWN database — never let two services share a database directly (that recreates tight coupling through the back door).

Inter-Service Communication

Synchronous (REST/gRPC):  Service A calls Service B, WAITS for a response.
  → Simple to reason about, but couples A's availability to B's — if B is slow/down, A suffers too
    (cascading failure — mitigate with timeouts, circuit breakers, bulkheads).

Asynchronous (events via Kafka/queue):  Service A publishes an event, doesn't wait.
  → A and B are decoupled in TIME — B can be down/slow without blocking A.
  → Trade-off: eventual consistency, harder to reason about/debug ("what happened, and when?").

Distributed Transactions — the SAGA Pattern

// A single ACID transaction across multiple services' separate databases doesn't exist. The SAGA
// pattern chains a sequence of LOCAL transactions, each with a COMPENSATING action to undo it:
1. Order Service:    create order (PENDING)         → compensate: cancel order
2. Payment Service:  charge card                      → compensate: refund
3. Inventory Service: reserve stock                    → compensate: release stock
// If step 3 fails, run compensations for steps 2 and 1 IN REVERSE — "undo" rather than "rollback."
// Two coordination styles: CHOREOGRAPHY (services react to each other's events, no central coordinator —
// simpler but harder to see the whole flow) vs ORCHESTRATION (a central saga coordinator directs each
// step explicitly — easier to reason about, but a new central component to build/scale).

Resilience Patterns (assume failure, design for it)

PatternProtects against
TimeoutA slow dependency hanging your service indefinitely
Circuit BreakerRepeatedly calling a KNOWN-failing dependency, wasting resources — "opens" after N failures, fails fast, periodically retries
BulkheadOne dependency's failure exhausting ALL your thread/connection pool, starving unrelated calls
Retry with backoff + jitterTransient failures — but NEVER retry non-idempotent calls blindly (rest-serverless lesson)

Interview Follow-ups to Expect

• How do you decide where to draw service boundaries? (bounded contexts around business capabilities, not technical layers)
• How do you handle a transaction spanning three services? (Saga — orchestrated or choreographed, with compensating actions)
• What happens when Service B is down and Service A depends on it synchronously? (timeout + circuit breaker to fail fast, ideally combined with a fallback or async decoupling)
